My Approach

How I
Think &
Work

I've run architecture reviews across dozens of platforms and built production systems from scratch. Here's the thinking that connects both ends of that spectrum.

01
Understand the real problem
Most AI failures are problem-framing failures. Before touching a model, I spend time on what the business actually needs vs. what they think they need.
02
Build a working version first
Architecture hypotheses need to be tested against real data and real failure modes. I prototype before I prescribe — the gap between diagram and reality is where most projects fail.
03
Design for the team, not just the system
A technically perfect architecture that no one can operate is a liability. I design with the actual team's capabilities in mind — and build the capability gap into the delivery plan.
04
Govern without killing velocity
The best governance frameworks I've built were ones teams actually wanted to follow — because they removed friction, not added it.
05
Share what I learn
If something I built can save someone else 3 weeks, that's more valuable than keeping it proprietary. Open contribution is a professional obligation, not just a hobby.

Agentic Architecture

Building Multi-Agent Systems That Actually Govern Themselves

The hardest part of agentic AI isn't getting the first agent to work — it's making 10 agents work together without chaos. This means designing clear responsibility boundaries, implementing tool-use guardrails aligned with OWASP Top 10 for LLM security, and building observability into every layer so you can see what the system is "thinking" before it acts. Auto-governance patterns can eliminate manual approval bottlenecks entirely when the trust framework is designed right.

RAG Optimization

Why Most RAG Pipelines Fail at Scale

Naive RAG works in demos. Production RAG needs intentional chunking strategies, hybrid search, re-ranking, and evaluation frameworks that measure what actually matters — not just embedding similarity scores. The eval loop is the part most teams skip, and it's the part that bites them.

LLM Cost Architecture

Dual-Gateway LLM Routing for Cost & Latency Control

Running one frontier model for everything is expensive and slow. A dual-gateway pattern routes by complexity — simple queries to smaller, faster models, complex reasoning to frontier models — while maintaining full security and auditability. The result: cost turns from a scaling problem into a routing problem, and latency meets SLA by design rather than by luck.
